A Personal SDLC That Scales With My Creativity

The Gap In My Personal Workflow

In enterprise engineering, we rely on structured delivery frameworks because they scale across teams, timelines, and technologies. But personal projects rarely get the same discipline. The result is predictable: dozens of half‑started ideas and no clear sense of where anything stands. I honestly lost interest and stopped working on some projects becaues I just couldn't keep track of where I was and where I'd left off the last time I was working on it, and how I'd ever get them to "done". I wanted to track my personal projects with a lightweight SDLC that mirrors real software evolution without the overhead of formal process — something aligned with the way I naturally approach building systems.

Phases

The lifecycle I landed on is simple:

Idea → Exploring → Building → Live → Evolving/Legacy

Each phase represents a real psychological and technical state I move through.

  • Idea captures the spark.
  • Exploring lets me test feasibility without commitment.
  • Building is where the system becomes real, while acknowledging the architectural groundwork that always precedes meaningful progress.
  • Live is the release milestone — the moment a project becomes a real artifact in my ecosystem.
  • Evolving represents active development after launch.
  • Legacy represents long‑term maintenance without new features: framework and dependency upgrades, security patches. It’s not abandonment — it’s stewardship.

The most important shift is what happens after a project goes Live. Traditional personal workflows treat “Done” as the final state, but nothing we build is ever truly done. Even stable projects require maintenance, dependency upgrades, and the occasional security patch. Splitting the post‑launch world into Evolving and Legacy gives me a more honest way to track the long tail of a project’s life.

Difference From Enterprise SDLC

Even though this lifecycle resembles enterprise SDLC on the surface, its purpose is fundamentally different. Enterprise frameworks exist to coordinate teams, manage risk, and enforce predictable delivery across large systems. My personal SDLC exists for an entirely different reason: to manage cognitive load, track momentum across many parallel ideas, and give myself an honest way to understand where each project sits in its natural evolution. The structure is familiar because it’s a good structure, but the intent is lighter, more adaptive, and optimized for how I actually build — not for governance, compliance, or cross‑team alignment. In other words, it borrows the shape of enterprise SDLC, but it’s designed for clarity, not coordination.

A Structure That Scales With Me

The problem was never that I didn’t finish some projects — it was that I was using the wrong definition of “finished” and I wasn’t heading toward that state in a smart way. By adopting a personal SDLC that mirrors the realities of enterprise engineering, I’ve created a structure that scales with my creativity instead of fighting it. It’s simple, honest, and sustainable. It’s already reshaping how I build, and it's already made me more productive.
