Engineering and Creativity

I’ve learned over time that the hardest engineering problems usually aren’t blocked by lack of technical skill, they’re blocked by how we’re framing the problem. Early in my career, I’d hear things like “this is hard to test” or “that’s just how the system works” and take them at face value. Now I treat those moments as signals that we’re missing a seam, a boundary, or a better abstraction. That shift, from solving what’s in front of us to stepping back and asking what the problem really is, is where creativity shows up for me. It’s not about being clever; it’s about questioning the shape of the problem before locking in a solution.

That’s why I’m drawn to the space where engineering and creativity overlap. Creative thinking is what turns a one-off bug fix into a reusable test or guardrail that prevents the whole class of failures from happening again. It’s what turns “modernize this app” into building something other teams actually want to adopt because it makes their work easier. And it’s what turns incident reviews into real changes in how teams collaborate, not just promises to be more careful next time.

The teams I want to build aren’t the ones that just move fast on the current task. They’re the ones that slow down just enough to make sure they’re solving the right problem, try small experiments to learn quickly, and design systems that leave things better for the next team than they found them.

And that, to me, is what engineering leadership is: applied creativity with a build pipeline behind it.
